The Newton-Cotes Integration Formulae

October 19, 2024  ● 2 mins read

The general idea behind the Newton-Cotes integration formulae,

$$\begin{equation} \label{gformula} I=\int_{a}^{b}f(x)dx \approx \int_{a}^{b}f_{n}(x)dx \end{equation}$$

where \(f_{n}(x)\) is the \(n^{th}\) order interpolating polynomial

$$f_{n}(x) = a_0 + a_1x + \ldots + a_{n-1}x^{n-1} + a_nx^n$$

Using, Lagrange’s interpolating polynomial \(L(x)\), such that \(L(x) = f_n(x)\)

$$ \begin{equation} L(x) = l_0(x)f(x_0) + l_1(x)f(x_1) + \ldots + l_{n}(x)f(x_{n}), \end{equation} $$

where \(l_0(x),l_1(x),\ldots,l_{n}(x)\) represent Langrange’s fundamental polynomials
Substituing \(L(x)\) into \(\ref{gformula}\)

$$\begin{align} \label{derivation} I=\int_{a}^{b}f(x)dx \approx \int_{a}^{b}L(x)dx &= \int_{a}^{b}\left(\sum_{i=0}^{n}l_i(x)f(x_i)\right)dx\\ &=\sum_{i=0}^{n}f(x_i)\underbrace{\int_a^b l_i(x) \, dx}_{w_i} \end{align}$$

Therefore,

$$\begin{equation} \label{quadformula} I=\int_{a}^{b}f(x)dx \approx \sum_{i=0}^nw_if(x_i), \end{equation}$$

This is the general form of the Newton-Cotes quadrature formulae

Definition: General Newton-Cotes Quadrature

$$\begin{equation} \label{ncform} I=\int_{a}^{b}f(x)dx \approx \sum_{i=0}^nw_if(x_i), \end{equation}$$ where $w_i$ are the weights, which can be computed as the integrals of Lagrange's fundamental polynomials

The Newton-Cotes quadrature methods can be classified into the open as well as the closed form

Definition: Closed Form

Data points at the beginning as well as the end of the limits of integration are known $$\begin{equation} \label{ncclosed} y_{i+1} = y_{i-n+1} + \int_{x_{i-n+1}}^{x_{i+1}}f_n(x)dx \end{equation}$$

Definition: Open Form

Limits of integration extend beyond the range of data $$\begin{equation} \label{ncopen} y_{i+1} = y_{i-n} + \int_{x_{i-n}}^{x_{i+1}}f_n(x)dx, \end{equation}$$ in such a case, the integral over the rest of the function segments is computed through extrapolation

Trapezoidal Rule#

It is a closed form of the Newton-Cotes quadrature
Can be obtained by substituting $n=1$ in $\ref{ncclosed}$

Derivation: Trapezoidal Rule, using Lagrange's interpolating polynomial

$$\begin{equation} \label{gentrap} I = \int_a^bf(x)dx \approx \int_a^bf_1(x)dx \end{equation}$$ where $f_1(x)$ is the first order Lagrange interpolating polynomial $$\begin{align*} f_1(x) &= f(a) + \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}(x-a)\\ &=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}x + f(a) - \frac{af(b)-af(a)}{b-a} \\ \vdots \\ &= \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a} + \frac{bf(a)-af(b)}{b-a}\\ \end{align*}$$ substituting $f_1(x)$ in \ref{gentrap} $$\begin{align} I &= \left.\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\frac{x^2}{2}\right|_a^b + \left.\frac{bf(a)-af(b)}{b-a}x\right|_a^b\\ &= \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\left(\frac{b^2-a^2}{2}\right) + \frac{bf(a)-af(b)}{b-a}(b-a)\\ \vdots \\ I &= \underbrace{\left(\frac{b-a}{2}\right)}_{h}[f(a)+f(b)], \end{align}$$ which fits into the forms described in both $\ref{ncform}$ and $\ref{ncclosed}$

Simpson’s $1/3^{rd}$ Rule#

The trapezoidal rule gives very high error for higher order polnomials, to rectify this problem Simpson’s rule employs a 2nd order interpolating polynomial

Derivation: Simpson's Rule, using Lagrange's Interpolation

$$\begin{equation} \label{gensimp1} I = \int_{x_0}^{x_2}f(x)dx \approx \int_{x_0}^{x_2}f_2(x)dx \end{equation}$$ where $f_2(x)$ is Lagrange's second order interpolating polynomial $$x_0 = a, x_2 = b$$ $$\begin{align*} f_2(x) = \frac{(x-x_1)(x-x_2)}{(x_0-x_1)(x_0-x_2)}f(x_0) + \frac{(x-x_0)(x-x_2)}{(x_1-x_0)(x_1-x_2)}f(x_1) \\+ \frac{(x-x_0)(x-x_1)}{(x_2-x_0)(x_2-x_1)}f(x_2) \end{align*}$$ substituting $f_1(x)$ in \ref{gensimp1} $$\begin{align*} I \approx \int_{x_0}^{x_2}\left[\frac{(x-x_1)(x-x_2)}{(x_0-x_1)(x_0-x_2)}f(x_0) + \frac{(x-x_0)(x-x_2)}{(x_1-x_0)(x_1-x_2)}f(x_1)\right. \\+ \left.\frac{(x-x_0)(x-x_1)}{(x_2-x_0)(x_2-x_1)}f(x_2)\right] \end{align*}$$ $$\begin{equation} I \approx \frac{h}{3}[f(x_0) + 4f(x_1) + f(x_2)] \end{equation}$$
